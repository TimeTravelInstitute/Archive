Other Myths surrounding Thoth:

Thoth has played a prominent role in many of the Egyptian myths. Displaying his role as arbitrator, he had overseen the three epic battles between good and evil. All three battles are fundamentally the same and belong to different periods. The first battle took place between Ra and Apep, the second between Heru-Bekhutet and Set, and the third between Horus, the son of Osiris, and Set. In each instance, the former god represented order while the latter represented chaos. If one god was seriously injured, Thoth would heal them to prevent either from overtaking the other.
<font color="white">Thoth also is reported to arrive every thousand years. [/COLOR]
Thoth was also prominent in the Osiris myth, being of great aid to Isis. After Isis gathered together the pieces of Osiris' dismembered body, he gave her the words to resurrect him so she could be impregnated and bring forth Horus. When Horus was slain, Thoth gave the formulae to resurrect him as well. Similar to God speaking the words to create the heavens and Earth in Judeo-Christian mythology, Thoth, being the god who always speaks the words that fulfill the wishes of Ra, spoke the words that created the heavens and Earth in Egyptian mythology.

This mythology also credits him with the creation of the 365 day calendar. Originally, according to the myth, the year was only 360 days long and Nut was sterile during these days, unable to bear children. Thoth gambled with Khonsu, the moon, for 1/72nd of its light (360/72 = 5), or 5 days, and won. During these 5 days, Nut gave birth to Kheru-ur (Horus the Elder, Face of Heaven), Osiris, Set, Isis, and Nepthys.

In the Ogdoad cosmogony myth, Thoth gave birth to Ra, Atum, Nefertum, and Khepri.

Greetings Kerr,

Well, there is certainly no doubt in my mind that Thoth is certainly a much more exciting potential time traveler than John Titor (and that means he is light years ahead of HDRKID!)

However, the problem, of course, is always in separating myth from fact. On a small scale this is the same problem we have with Titor (and on a miniscule scale the issue with HDRKID). Perhaps the really instructive aspect then is not so much "is/was someone really a time traveler" but rather "what role does myth play in the human experience?" Two questions that come to my mind in this regard:

1) What is it about the human psyche that causes people to "want" (choose) to believe in something that sound clearly mythical?
2) Is there a bigger purpose for myth and mysticism other than just "relating facts of what occurred in the past"?

In mythical fables we see important lessons that are intended to get people to consider the ramifications of their actions (or inactions, as the case may be). We know the story of the tortoise and the hare, and the story of the ant and the grasshopper. Clearly, no one in their right mind would claim these were accurate accountings of history... for we understand they are intended to teach lessons. So why is it that humans seems to ascribe a higher level of "believability" when a myth is inscribed within the construct of a human life?

I often think one of the greatest minds of our past century to grapple with the "Power of Myth" was none other than Joseph Campbell. I believe he helped us understand that the instance of a myth wherein the mythical figure was human was a vehicle for transmitting knowledge over time. In other words, wrapped within the myth were aspects of truth that a particular culture felt was important to pass down to later generations... and without books or computers, the myth was a perfect vehicle to do just that.

Other thoughts on the power and purpose of myths in our own lives?

Time Traveler Thoth in the America's ?

It is interesting to note the resemblance of the Quetzacoatl legend with that of the myth of the Pahana held by the Hopis of northern Arizona. Scholars have described many similarities between the myths of the Aztecs and those of the American Southwest.

The Hopi describe the Pahana as the "Lost White Brother," and they expected his eventual return from the east during which he would destroy the wicked and begin a new era of peace and prosperity. Hopi tradition maintains that they at first mistook the Spanish conquistadors as the Pahana when they arrived on the Hopi mesas in the 16th century.

Some scholars still hold the view that the fall of the Aztec empire can in part be attributed to Moctezuma's belief in Corts as the returning Quetzalcoatl.

Quetzalcoatl was called "Ipalnemohuani", a title reserved for the gods directly involved in the creation, which means "by whom we live". Because the name Ipalnemohuani is singular, this led to speculations that the Aztec were becoming monotheistic and all the main gods were only one.

Quetzalcoatl was said to oppose human sacrifice.

Quetzalcoatl is often referred to as the patron god of the Aztec priesthood, of learning and knowledge.

Quetzalcoatl is often referred to as The Feathered Serpent.

The Sacred Ibis was an object of religious veneration in ancient Egypt, particularly associated with the god, Thoth .
