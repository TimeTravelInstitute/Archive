TT23,

There are several theories of quantum mechanics that generally fall under the "Many Worlds Interpretation" umbrella.

Rather than have people point out websites why don't you do some research on your own and then decide what you want to include.

Google the following:

"Everett Relative State Formalism"

"Relative State Formalism" (of quantum mechanics) was the title of Hugh Everett's PhD dissertation. John Wheeler was his graduate advisor/PhD sponsor. That is the basis of "Many Worlds Interpretation" or "multiverses."

If you spend some time doing the research you might find yourself a bit disappointed, however. Everett wasn't talking about the sci-fi "parallel worlds" idea. John Wheeler has called "Many Worlds" an unfortunate choice of words because it is misleading and has caused a lot of confusion.

But you need to do the research rather than taking my word or someone else's word on this. You need to "hear" it from the horses' mouths, so to speak.

You should also Google the same terms on Google, Groups, Science, Physics, Research (SPR). You'll see UseNet posts from working physicists on the subject.

TT23,

Just a short follow up:

As you research Many Worlds Interpretation (MWI) you'll run across The Copenhagen Interpretation of Quantum Mechanics (Niels Bohr) and The Bohmian Interpretation of Quantum Mechanics (David Bohm).

If you get into Bohmian Interpretation you might as well get a copy of "The Theory of Special Relativity" by David Bohm. You should have some background knowledge on Special Relativity and Bohm's book is wonderful. Not too much math and most of the math is basic algebra.

Also get a copy of Einstein's "Relativity: The Special and General Theories." It is a popular science paperback written by Einstein himself and is intended for the general public.

Both books are available on Amazon.com and Border's Bookstore.

You need to have some knowledge of the background that leads to quantum mechanics. And learning is fun in any case. Niels Bohr, Albert Einstein and David Bohm plus a few more physicists are the true "fathers" of modern physics.
