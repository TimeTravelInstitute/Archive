OK... I've been lurking here for a few weeks. I don't believe the whole Titor thing... but I'm going to add some fuel to the fire there

Dennis le Petite

According to the Gregorian calendar, the millennium starts on Jan.1,2001, because the calendar places Jesus Christ's birth at year 01 A.D. not zero. So, if a millennium is a thousands years, then the first millennium lasted from the year 1 to the end of the year 1000, the second began at the start of the year 1001 and ends Dec.31, 2000.

The problem stems from the work of Dionysius Exiguus, a.k.a. Dennis the Little, a sixth-century monk. The pope of that time gave Dennis the task of reconciling the calendars of the Eastern and Western Churches to figure out the proper day for celebrating Easter. Using the Roman calendar, Dennis counted backward to detemine the date of the first Easter. Then, he reset the way years were numbered to correspond with Jesus' birth. Before that, years were measured either from the founding of Rome or from the ascension of the emperor.

Dennis fixed Jesus' birth as 1 A.D. (Anno Domini, "the year of our Lord") becuose in the mathematics of that time, zero didn't exist; the Western world was introduced to it a few centuries later by the Arabs.

That's why the third millennium starts Jan.01.2001. Except, there's still another problem with Dennis' calculatons.

When Dennis worked backward to fix the date of Jesus' birth, he actually miscalculated by a few yaers. Biblical scholars now figure Jesus' actual date of birth was sometime between 7 B.C. and 4 B.C.; putting the start of the third millennium sometime between 1994 and 1997.

That three-year window was set by matching dates to the Bible story. The Bible tells us Jesus was born during the reign of king Herod. According to the first-century historian Josephus, Herod died shortly before the annual Jewish Passover and shortly after a lunar eclipse. Astronomers know that there were eclipses in 5 B.C., 4 B.C. and 1 B.C. The consensus is that Herod died in 4 B.C. So that puts Jesus birth before 4 B.C.

There is still another problem if you were intent on celebrating the 2000th anniversary of Jesus' birth. Dennis ' calendar was supposed to be based on Jesus' birth. But, he started the New Year on Jan. 1, not the traditional Dec. 25, supposedly to coincide with Jesus' circumcision (but not coincidentally the same New Year date that was already celebrated on the Roman calendar).

Of course, Jesus wasn't actually born on Dec. 25. Scholars say it was likely picked by the Church to lure pagan converts who would have been celebrating the winter solstice around that time. Nobody knows the actual birth date................so, Happy millennium, whenever you pick to have it........
