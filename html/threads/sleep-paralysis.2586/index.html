First of all, prceptdboundry, sleep paralysis is a common occurrence which most people will experience at least once or twice in their lives. It is well documented, and pretty well understood, at least biochemically. There has been a fair amount of neurological study devoted to the phenomenon. It is acknowledge by the present scientific community as a natural phenomenon, and also historically by the names of Night Terrors, Succubi, and Hag Visitations. There is likely nothing paranormal about it. Here are some resources you may find useful:
http://www.arts.uwaterloo.ca/~acheyne/S_P.html
http://www.dreamsnightmares.com/sleepparalysis.html
http://www.nightterrors.org/.html

I myself have had numerous experiences with sleep paralysis. I conquered it. I'll explain what I mean in a moment.

The first instance I can remember occurred a few years ago when I was in high school. I was sleeping on the couch in my parents' living room. I had been reading one of Whitley Streiber's autobiographical alien abduction books just before I fell asleep, and had been in an extremely suggestible state due to how tired I was. The last chapter I had read before turning the light out was one about the terror Mr. Streiber had felt when paralyzed during his abduction experiences. The chapter had thoroughly spooked me. I turned the light off and quickly fell asleep. The next thing I was aware of was music playing. I still felt half asleep, but I could hear, as if far away and under water, the song Glycerine by Bush playing somewhere. I figured I had fallen asleep while listening to music through headphones, and so I tried to reach up and hit the stop button. My arm would not respond to my attempts. I tried and tried, but I could not move, and then I realized that the music was in my head! It was getting more and more sinister and distorted the more I paid attention to it. I started freaking out a little bit, and then became quite afraid when I noticed the sensation of a heavy weight sitting on my chest. It got heavier and heavier by the second, until I felt it was going to crush the life out of me. I tried to scream to wake myself up, and after several terrifying failed attempts, I screamed my fool lungs out, waking up everyone in the house.

After that, realizing what had happened, I decided I should not be afraid of the phenomenon. I experienced the paralysis a few more times, and was as startled and afraid of it as before. Once I had gotten used to it though, I was prepared.

On my fifth or six instance of paralysis, I managed to calm my mind and examine the experience objectively. I found that if I just kept breathing steadily and ignored the sensation of paralysis, I could control what I perceived as my movement around the room. In that state, everything looked slightly distorted and distinctly luminous, but quite accurate to waking life. I could even see my sleeping body in bed if I turned toward it. On this instance, I was joined by a little grinning purple devil face in profile, which stayed at the edge of my vision. It annoyed me, so I tried to send myself as far from my bedroom as possible. Instantly, I found myself out over the ocean, looking toward a large sandbar with a stand of tropical trees growing on it. I felt like I was snared in a thick black net. I could not get through it, so I woke up.

The next time I experienced sleep paralysis, I not only controlled it while it was happening, but invited it upon myself while awake. I lay down for a nap in the afternoon (the time at which I was most prone to S.P.), and told myself "I want to have sleep paralysis during this nap". Sure enough, just a few minutes after falling asleep, I woke up paralyzed and apparently lying on the pillow beside my head. I felt something sitting on my chest, but this time it was not crushing me, but instead squirming around helplessly. I looked down to see that it was a little translucent neon green and orange sloth, about six inches long. It was crawling up toward my head. It reached one claw up to my forehead and pulled out a tendril that was my own awareness, and drew it into itself. I started crawling around the bed as the little sloth. I crawled to the bedpost, gripped it, and slid down to the floor. Once there, I started reading a scroll which I could not really locate in space anywhere around me. There seemed to be a lot of text on it, but all I can remember is this: "We are slowly drifting toward the male, but the qubur is female". The text then turned into odd runes, then into a series of oscilloscope readouts. Then I woke up.

That was about three years ago, and I have not had sleep paralysis since. I feel like my determination to control it allowed me to conquer it. Since I don't fear the sensation anymore, my subconscious mind is no longer subtly drawn toward it by the same desire to be scared that makes one want to watch horror movies. I now kind of miss S.P. It's sort of a shame that it stopped just as I was getting the hang of it. It was becoming like a game, and I have to wonder just how far I could have taken it. I don't know if I believe in such things as the "astral body", and I know I don't believe in demonic possession or the existence of a devil. But still, perhaps I could have gained some useful insights into the operation of my own mind.

p.s.: A hasty internet search for "qubur" yielded links to a few sites that are apparently in arabic or some related dialect. Based upon context, I infer that the word means something like 'grave', or 'headstone'. Incidentally, I have no idea what the sentence from the text might mean.

Yeah, what he said. It's a very common, widley known event that is well understood. I've not looked into it for a few years but, if memory serves, the cause is simply to do with the way that the brain operates.

As we fall asleep, the brain blocks certain signals to the body so that, for example, when we dream that we are walking our legs don't actually make the motions. In rare instances, especially when we are falling asleep, or just waking, this can happen before we lose consiousness or remain for a time after we regain consiousness. And it's that simple.

It's happened to me, too and it is disconcerting. However, as your mind is partically consious, you are in control. If you are armed with the knowledge of what Sleep Paralysis actually is, then you should be able to overcome any fear you may have, in the same way that if you realise that you're having a nightmare it is no longer scary (well, I'm never scared by my nightmares these days in any case. Of course, naturally, no two people are going to have precisely similar experiences of sleep paralysis (or dreaming, or waking life, for that matter).

Though I was not exactly wide awake as you were during bouts of S.P., prceptboundry, I was certainly in a state which could not rightly be called sleep. When I say I "woke up" from sleep paralysis, what I really mean to say is that I returned to a state of normal waking consciousness. It's just easier to say that I "woke up" as a sort of shorthand. During S.P., my mind was quite awake, though in a highly abnormal state and rife with visual, tactile, and auditory hallucinations, but my body remained asleep. When I said that I tried to wake myself up, what I meant more precisely was that I tried to wake my body up, i.e., tried to regain my mind's conscious control over my body's movements. From what I've researched, my experiences were more or less classic, by-the-book S.P.

As for your experiences, that is quite unusual indeed for someone to experience S.P. with their minds wide awake! I've always found that the most frustrating thing about S.P. is that if I think too hard about it while it's happening, I'll either fall back to sleep or fully wake up. Remaing in that state where my mind seems to wander about detatched from my body is like walking a very thin edge, where the slightest wrong move makes me slide down one side or the other.
