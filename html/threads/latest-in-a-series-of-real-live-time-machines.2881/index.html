Interesting thoughts, Creedo. But a lot of what you say is desireable would not be justified by a formal engineering analysis of the problem. And indeed, some of the things you say are not at all represented in the well-designed, second generation Roomba Discovery SE.

NOTE to Creedo and Time Travelers, this board and others: The Roomba is brought to us as an outgrowth of the successful MIT Artificial Intelligence labs. The company called irobot was started by several principals from the MIT AI Lab. So be very aware....they know what they are doing, and it shows in their products.

Most good house-bot will have a stable base, so the nicads, or any sort of rechargeable battery, would be placed close to the floor.
Click to expand...
Yes, true.

The wheels and motors moving servos, should have a push weight of about fifty pounds, which is desirable.
Click to expand...
Not true. This force level is too high, as it could result in the tipping over of light, and/or vertically destablized objects in the home. Furthermore, a motor that provides a 50 pound push force would be quite heavy.

There should be a self-plug drogue feature, for the robot to recharge itself, at the end of the day?
Click to expand...
A drogue plug would be highly inefficient, as this would drive an unnecessary precision docking requirement. The Roomba solution is much more elegant: Two small, hemispherical metal terminals mounted on a small, partial circle docking circle which provides a low-power RF localizing signal which the Roomba can find the null point of. The Roomba simply centers on the RF beam, and performs a complete 180 degree turn, and then backs onto the dock, causing the battery pack's charge terminals to mate with the docking station's terminals. Works great, without the complexity of a drogue device.

B.The chassis should be built of aluminum or fiberglass and ridged and strtudy enough to support servo arms, which have a pick-up ability, for twenty pounds, at least.
Click to expand...
Naaah. The robovac should not "pick up" anything other than dust, hair, and small debris. It should not have enough power to suck up any single object that weighs more than about 2 ounces.

For communications, and I.R. series of nodes, or frequency charts, can be strung through-out the house
Click to expand...
Once again, you are not thinking like a systems engineer, Creedo. IR has obvious line-of-sight problems. The sensors are more expensive, and less rugged than simple spread-spectrum RF.

The Roomba comes with two "keep out" RF modules that are powered by 2 D-cell batteries. You turn them on and place them inside a room pointing along a line parallel to the door. The Roomba senses this beam and does not cross over it, and thereby stays out of the room.

D.A series of warning lights, flashing LED lights, and or warning beepers, can be added to this housebot?
Click to expand...
Yup. Has some really cute sequences that it plays when its mission is accomplished, and it returns to its docking pad.

E.This housebot should be sealed, to withstand weather, if accidentally let out and can for some reason, can not get back inside.
Click to expand...
Nope. Again, these requirements would drive the design to be too large and too heavy. The Roomba is quite effective, but they tell you in the documentation that it is an "indoor pet".

You would make a good systems engineer, but you need more training in understanding the relationships between Operational, Functional, and Physical design domains. I am just wrapping up my ARO 201 course: Fundamentals of Systems Engineering at Cal Poly where I teach precisely these skills.
