To be good in robotics it would make sense to get a good grounding in complex systems engineering tools and practices. It is a bit funny, but Microsoft seems to have co-opted the words "systems engineer" to mean something quite a bit more limited than what conventional systems engineering is really about. When someone says they are "MCSE certified" it means they know how to implement Microsoft tools in an IT environment. But a practicing systems engineer has to have knowledge in a large cross-section of science and engineering disciplines. Robotics demands this same sort of depth.

For example, if you look at a UAV as an autonomous robot (which is exactly what it is), there are the following types of systems typically involved in its makeup:

1) Aerodynamic systems (obviously).
2) Mechanical systems (cables, rods, wheels, etc.)
3) Hydraulic systems
4) Electrical power systems
5) Fuel systems
6) Vision/sensor systems (for seeing/sensing the world around the vehicle)
7) Guidance Navigation, & Control systems (software, intelligence)
8) Even (sometimes) weapon systems! (The fun stuff, eh Freud?)

The ability to analyze system operational needs, design system functional capabilities to fulfill those needs, and design the physical systems listed above that execute those functions is the domain of the systems engineer.

You would do well to study and understand the postings I have made relating to the Operational-Functional-Physical triplex domains of information. Understanding them, and how to use them in doing design analysis and definition will take you far in systems engineering.

Good luck and have fun. Since you know I am an engineering educator, do not ever hesitate to ask questions. I'd be more than happy to help!
