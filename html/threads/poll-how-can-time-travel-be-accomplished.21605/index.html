What is the fastest moving thing in this dimension, the thing which nothing moves faster than, the thing which moves at the same speed relative to you regardless of how fast you're moving? Light. Now light has a velocity of 186,000 mph in this dimension, but it may have different velocities in other dimensions. We also have to discover a way to return to, or at least interact with, this dimension; however I have a hunch that doing that may be far less complicated.

...which is a huge problem.

We know, as a matter of experimental verification for the past century, that for massive particles they are limited to velocities less than c. At the speed of light their mass and momentum blow up to infinity. Mix in quantum mechanics and you know long before that can consider matching the speed of light you simply don't have sufficient local energy available to form the next quanta of energy for the accelaration boost to get to a velocity that is still less than c.

Now you can imagine another universe where the laws of physics are different than they are in this universe. String Theory (or rather its current form, M-Theory) has literally billions of solutions that neither describe this universe nor violate the known laws of physics. They even allow for different laws of physics in different universes. The problem for us, even if we could somehow enter those universes, is that the laws of physics that allow for our form of matter would not apply there. If, for example, the strength of the electric charge was even slightly different in that universe our molecules would either collapse or disintegrate. If the strong force was different the atoms in our bodies could fly apart or collapse to black holes. If gravity was even slightly stronger stars in that universe would burn out long before sufficient time has passed for life to form. If it was weaker stars wouldn't form at all.

Asking for different laws of physics involves all of physics and all of the effects that would result. A theory of time travel involving those universes means exploring the entirety of what would happen. Having your electrons, protons and neutrons individually stream off into the future/past might verify a time travel theory but (1) you wouldn't like the experience having died and (2) no one would know that it was a success because there would be no one to report back the results.

As to possibly accelerating to some velocity near c in this universe and them transiting to a universe where the limit is lower - thus we exceed the speed of light in that universe...no go. It won't allow you to time travel relative to this universe.

We can and do accelerate particles to velocities where they exceed the local speed of light. We do it all the time. The speed limit that you're talking about is the speed of light in a vacuum. We can't match that velocity with massive particles. But if you accelerate a particle in an accelerator to 99.999999% the speed of light and then direct the particle into heavy water, Viola! The particle is exceeding the local light speed limit - you'll even see Cherenkov Radiation if you shoot enough particles into the heavy water. That's the blue-green glow you see in reactors. Unfortunately, the particles stay rather firmly attached to this time and don't disappear into the past though they do, in fact, experience time dilation as described in Special Relativity.
