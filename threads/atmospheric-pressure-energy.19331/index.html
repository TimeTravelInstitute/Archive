Head pressure in any fluid is a pretty easy concept to model, based on the hydrostatic relationships. The head pressure for any fluid can be expressed in terms of the height of a column of that fluid, independent of the shape of the column at either end. For any height of a fluid "h" we can compute its head pressure over that height as-head = (Fluid Density)*g*(fluid height) P = rho*g*h

So whatever the height of the device you wish to build, we can model the head pressure difference between the bottom and the top with respect to the height (h) between them.

The energy equation written in terms of fluid pressure could be represented as:

P-static + P-head + P-dynamic = Constant. OR

P-static1 + Phead1 + P-dynamic1 = P-static2 + Phead2 + P-dynamic2

P-static2 = 10.10 lb/in^s (1455.2 lb/ft^2)

P-dynamic1 = 0.5*density1*Velocity1^2

P-dynamic2 = 0.5*density2*Velocity2^2

P-head1 = density1*g*h1 (since we define h1= 0 feet, this term goes away. All head pressures are then referenced to Mean Sea Level)

P-head2 = density2*g*h2

We can assume "g" is constant since it changes by a VERY small amount over the 10,000 feet we are interested in. And we can also use the ICAO standard atmosphere to tell us what the air densities are at out two altitudes on a standard, temperate day:

density1 = .002377 slugs/ft^3

So now we are down to two unknowns (Velocity1 and Velocity2), but only one equation. The other equation we can use is that for steady flow the mass flow rate must be constant. The equation for mass flow rate for a compressible fluid is:

m-dot = density1*Area1*Velocity1 = density2*Area2*Velocity2

I'd encourage you to solve these equations for one of the velocities (either the one at the top or bottom, doesn't much matter) and more importantly to program the solution into a tool like MS-Excel so you can study how things change as you change the pressure and velocity at one end.
