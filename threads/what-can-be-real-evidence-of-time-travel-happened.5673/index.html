Known ancient and other texts/manuscripts have been examined and studied in depth by many scholars. I would imagine what you are suggesting would have been found already. Unless these words or descriptions were encoded within said texts.

I would like to bring up Nostradamus as "my" example. Claiming to see visions of the future, his descriptions are written with the "vocabulary" of his time.

Only after an event ( seems familiar ) do some people suddenly proclaim his "predictions" to have been accurate.

After the fact, anybody can make all kinds of proclaimation's regarding those kind of writings, since there is a wide margin for interpretation as to what his words mean(t).

If Nostradamus was in contact with a time traveler, his ability to record exactly what the time traveler told him, seems a bit lacking. And this points out another problem --- even if a time traveler used such words in ancient times, the listener is still confined within the vocabulary of experience of his era.

And it wouldn't be just the word that would have to be known, but also the context in which the word was used. Who knows, maybe there was an ancient civilization that wrote the word "internet" within a text regarding the techniques of using a "fishing net". Anybody flipping through the pages of the text, without knowing what the "entire" text is about, might mistakenly believe they have discovered proof of time travel within the text.

Mother Shipton lived in the 16th century

The first known edition of Mother Shipton's prophecies appeared in print in 1641, eighty years after her death. However, the most important editions of her work appeared in 1684, edited by Richard Head (this edition included the earliest biographical information about her), and in 1862, edited by Charles Hindley.

Mother Shipton's prophecies are hoaxes, because it now appears that almost all of them were written by others after the events they described had already happened. For instance, the first record of her prophecy about Cardinal Wolsey dates from 1641, long after the man had died. Her prophecies about future technology, and about the world coming to an end in 1881, first appeared in print in the 1862 edition of her sayings, and Charles Hindley, the editor of that edition, later admitted that he had composed them.

The existence of Mother Shipton herself is uncertain. Her 1684 biographer, Richard Head, apparently invented most of the details of her life. In fact, she may never have existed outside of Yorkshire legend.
