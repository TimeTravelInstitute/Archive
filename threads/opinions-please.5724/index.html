Your first problem is in simple definitions.

GENETIC MEMORY
If you mean "memory" being stored in the brain using the DNA / mRNA system or similar, so that the brain is like a hard drive storing computer files- and we inherit some of those files from our ancestors- that doesn't seem controversial. Not as well proven as some seem to think, but not controversial.

REINCARNATION
"Reincarnation" can cover a lot of ground. Again, if you mean that one explanation for reincarnation experiences could be the accessing by the brain of previously untouched genetic memory- it sounds more reasonable than an infinite continuum of souls endlessly reincarnating between different physical bodies. There would likely be a sliver more proof substantiating some form of genetic memory theory than for reincarnation too.

Angelo,


Doghead is on point with his post.

Though I didn't use my degree directly in my professional life my undergrad university degree was in the field of experimental psychology. The idea of genetic memory is such that it lends itself to direct experimental testing and verification. One can design a proper experiment to test for genetic memory and confirm or refute the hypothesis. Reincarnation, on the other hand, isn't something that one can design a proper experiment for testing the hypothesis.

That's always been the problem with Duke U's Rhine Institute where they study the "paranormal". The students claim that they get positive experimental results but when you look closely at both the experimental design and the experimenters themselves you discover that the entire process is seriously flawed - mostly with experimenter bias. If you're a student at a large, very expensive private university are you going to spend your hard earned money (more likely mom and dad's hard earned money) entering a program in parapsychology as a skeptic knowing full well that your degree, if you get one, won't get you a cup of coffee at Denny's? Of course not. The only people who enter the program are people who are "believers" - and they're very defensive on the subject. It doesn't serve their sense of self esteem (and the credibility of the university degree that they are seeking) to discover that what they believe in has just been disproved by their own most recent experiment. It's been the case that when experiments "fail" they've had the tendency to blame it on flawed design rather than observing that the results were what they appeared to be - negative. When the results are positive they tend to ignore experimental design flaws that are pointed out.

The truth is that after over half a century they've yet to produce results that can be duplicated by an unbiased researcher. It fails the Scientific Method litmus test. Reincarnation is part of that area of "paranormal studies".
