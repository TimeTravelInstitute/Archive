Let's go back to your original post. I assume we are still talking about a spacecraft with a man inside it orbiting the Earth. There is a slight time dilation effect, 3.8 seconds in a year. You want to increase it's speed drastically to increase the time dilation effect.
First off, there are physical reasons you can't increase the speed a whole lot. An orbit is the result of two forces; gravity of the Earth pulling it down and it's speed which stops it falling. There is a relationship, a balance between these two that keeps it where it is.
Imagine you have a tennis ball on a thin string and you are swinging it around your head. You are the Earth, the ball is the ship and the string is gravity. You are swinging it hard enough to make a nice circle. The ball wants to escape; you can feel it. Now you start swinging it much faster and harder. The increased force overcomes the strength of the string; it breaks. The ball flies off into space.
Exactly the same will happen if you increase the speed of your space ship. It will escape the gravity and break orbit and fly off into space (but as Einstein says, it will enjoy even better time dilation effects out there)

But lets for the sake of argument ignore all that and say that you CAN increase the speed and stay in the same orbit . Let's increase the speed by 100 x, an incredible amount. The time dilation becomes 380 seconds, or getting on for 6 and 1/2 minutes. The astronaut will be six and a half minutes younger than he would be if he had stayed on Earth.

But he hasn't done anything: he has sat in a tin can for a year looking out the window. He hasn't travelled in time per se.
