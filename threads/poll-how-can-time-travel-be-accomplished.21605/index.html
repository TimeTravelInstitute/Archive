What would an imaginary or complex value for mass physically represent?

Take E = mc^2

If the value for mass is negative then the square root of mc^2 is imaginary. Mass and energy are equivalent according to this simply stated equation. We have examples of negative energy thus the values for mc^2 must be imaginary according to the rules of math.

How can you have negative energy? It comes down to a vacuum solution. A vacuum isn't the absence of everything. It is the lowest energy state of a system. Take a bowling ball and roll it down a hill until it stops rolling. That' the lowest energy state - a vacuum solution. Now back away from the ball, say, 1 km. Now you have a different picture of the state of the ball. Yes, it rolled down the hill until it hit a flat area and stopped rolling. But when you pulled back for a wider view you then saw that it stopped rolling near a cliff. You've already set its potential energy at zero. When it eventually rolls off the cliff it ends up at a yet lower energy state, a state that would be negative WRT where it was before falling off the cliff. It was a pseudo-vacuum state that you originally witnessed. But when you take the square root of delta-mc^2 (the change in energy after coming to rest after falling off the cliff) the value will be imaginary because the energy fell below zero. Subatomic particles can do the same thing even though the "hill" and "cliff" aren't physical objects.

Set c = 1 to normalize the equation. If c=1 then all of the squared values of "c" are just 1. Get rid of all of the c^2 references:

E®^2 = m(0)^2 + p^2

Setting c=1 is very common. C is a true constant and not frame dependent in special relativity.
