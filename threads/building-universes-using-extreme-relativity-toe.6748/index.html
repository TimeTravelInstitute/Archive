It's done all the time in physics. You'll see equations where the author notes that (s)he has used "normalized units". That means that the following universal (Planck) constants have been set to "1" in the equation: G (Gravitational Constant), h-bar (reduced Planck Constant), k _e(Coulomb Constant), K_b (Boltzmann's Constant) and c (Speed of light in a vacuum).

By setting them to 1 they have no effect on the equation other than to keep the units straight. So, in E=mc^2, if you set c = 1 what you have left is E = m. And that is what is meant by energy is equivalent to mass.

Using normalized units is not always appropriate, however. Imagine that you were working on the Manhattan Project during 1942-1944 and E=mc^2 was stated in normalized units in each and every equation used for the project (and each and every physicist and engineer was an idiot for not realizing it). You would have underestimated the energy released in a nuclear detonation by a factor of 90 billion.

Energy may be the equivalent of mass but energy is not the same physical thing as mass. The mass has to be multiplied by 90 billion (c^2) in order to see the relationship between mass and energy: 1 unit of mass is equal to 90 billion units of energy.

John, I understand your statements about directly measuring physics to fit into mathematics to make models for predictive purposes. But almost nothing in physics is directly observable. We build instrumentation that can repeatedly interact with some set of properties in an environment. There are no absolutes, we make approximations within the limits of measurements. Even quantum mechanics is based upon approximations as sets of probabilities.

For example:

You see your mother walking down the street. She is too far away to make out her facial features. But from the way she walks, the style of clothing, the direction she commonly travels, the approximate hair color, the length of the hair, the style of the hair, the apparent color of her skin, the approximate body proportions... she is described by the indirect set of observable properties and very few other people will fit into that total body of descriptions. You have reasonable confidence that the person you see walking is your mother; within some unstated probability.

In the sciences almost all measurements are done similarly. Even measuring a length with a gradient under a microscope. There are many properties of the environment (frequency of light, staining of sample, distance between gradient and sample, closeness to centers of microscope foci...) that are taken into consideration before a person will say with confidence that something is a certain length, within a stated probability (number of significant digits).

Gravity is not directly observable, but neither is your mother. The light that reflects off of your mother into your eyes varies depending upon an environment of a rich UV lightsource like a blacklight, and infrared; the limits of the human eye. UV has a shorter wavelength and objects will appear closer to you. Infrared the opposite. Artists take advantage of this phenomena to add 3 dimensional qualities to their 2D graphic designs. Your mind adjusts your observations for useful purpose. But certainly there are other people who look like your mother under certain conditions.

Almost all experimentation is accomplished by differences in outcomes, not direct observations. Direct observations in general don't provide useful experimental outcomes. I don't know of any exceptions off hand. Perhaps creating a simplified environment with known constraints, and then the only observations from those relative perspectives are known. But in physics, all the universe is connected so there are no measureable absolutes that can be measured with absolute exactness by a single direct observation.
