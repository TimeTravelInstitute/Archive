O.C. Deputies Taser, Strip Handcuffed Man on Video

SANTA ANA - A man who was arrested by Orange County sheriff's deputies was stripped of his pants, strapped to a chair, tasered multiple times while in handcuffs and slammed to the floor with a knee to his head, a newly released videotape shows.

The 13-minute video, given to The Associated Press by the man's attorney, was taken in March 2006 after 24-year-old Matthew Fleuret was arrested after a barroom brawl in San Clemente.

His attorney, Stephen Bernard, has filed a $47 million civil lawsuit against the county and recently obtained the grainy footage during preparation for that trial. Fleuret, a construction worker, was not charged and was released from custody within 24 hours, Bernard said Wednesday. He has no criminal record, his attorney said. Fleuret was not available to be interviewed, Bernard said.

Sheriff's spokesman Jim Amormino referred calls to Bill Haluck, an attorney for the county. Haluck did not return calls for comment.

The first portion of the video, which has no sound, shows Fleuret sitting handcuffed on a bench as he waits to be booked. A deputy walks up and appears to elbow Fleuret on the side of the head.

A few minutes later, the shirtless and handcuffed Fleuret is heard saying, ``I didn't do nothing,'' and requests to see an attorney.

The video then shows two deputies escorting Fleuret down a hallway, but as they approach the doorway to a medical unit Fleuret shouts ``No,'' followed by an expletive. He begins to struggle and at least six deputies swarm him and wrestle him to the ground.

Someone commands ``Tase him!'' and ``Tase him again!'' as Fleuret screams loudly. One deputy can be seen pinning his neck to the floor with his knee, while several other deputies appear to use their hands to hold his back and legs down.

Fleuret repeatedly screams, ``I can't breathe!'' as a deputy urges him to relax.

``The more you resist, the worse it gets,'' the deputy says.

Fleuret is then dragged into the medical facility and someone pulls down his jeans. Several other deputies hoist him into a chair, secure straps across his chest and place a black, transparent hood over his head - a ``spit guard'' to keep him from spitting at deputies.
